From 2D to High-Dimensional Embeddings
// The canvas above is a projection. Real embeddings live in 768–4096 dimensions. Here's what that actually means.
2D space — each word has 2 numbers: x, y

A dimension is just one number

An embedding is a list of floating-point numbers — one per dimension. In 2D you have two numbers: [x, y]. In 768D you have 768 numbers.


You can think of each number as measuring one axis of meaning. A 2D space might have axes for Royalty and Power. A 768D space has 768 axes — most are entangled combinations of concepts the model discovered by reading billions of words.


The canvas to the left shows 4 words in 2D. King and Queen are close. Sunset is far. That proximity is the whole point of embeddings.

Key idea: "Embedding" = a word encoded as a point in a geometric space. Two words that appear in similar contexts end up as nearby points. Distance in the space = difference in meaning.

Drag the slider to add more dimensions. Watch how each new dimension encodes a different aspect of meaning — and how the vectors for king, loan, and sunset look increasingly different from each other.

2
With only 2 dimensions, "bank" and "loan" are close. With more dimensions, the model can simultaneously encode semantic domain, syntactic role, formality, and hundreds of subtler features — all as directions in the same space.
2D: "bank" can only be in one place
In 2D, "bank" sits between finance and river — a compromise. In high-D it gets its own axis per sense, and context determines which direction activates.

2D forces trade-offs; high-D doesn't

The word "bank" means both a financial institution and a river bank. In 2D you can only put it in one place — halfway between both clusters, far from each.


With more dimensions the model gets separate axes for financial-context and geographical-context. Depending on the surrounding sentence, different dimensions activate.


More generally, English has millions of words and concepts. The relationships between them form a graph with millions of edges. You can't faithfully embed that graph in 2D without massive distortion. In 768D or 4096D, far fewer compromises are needed.

How do you visualise 768D?

You can't draw 768 axes on a screen. Instead, researchers use dimensionality reduction — algorithms that squash N-dimensional points down to 2 or 3 while preserving as much of the neighbourhood structure as possible.


The three most common techniques:

  • PCA — finds the 2 directions of maximum variance. Fast, linear, global structure preserved. Loses local clusters.
  • t-SNE — preserves local neighbourhoods well. Clusters pop out clearly. But distances between clusters are not meaningful.
  • UMAP — faster than t-SNE, preserves both local and some global structure. Current standard for embedding visualisation.

The canvas above demos uses [x, y] positions that were manually set to give an intuition — not computed from real model weights. The chart here shows a toy 8D→2D PCA projection so you can see the distortion involved.

Every 2D embedding plot you've seen in a paper, blog post, or demo — including the canvas at the top of this page — is a lossy projection. The true geometry lives in hundreds of dimensions.

More dims ≠ always better.
ada-002 at 1 536D beats BERT-large at 1 024D in most RAG benchmarks — but the difference is training data and loss function, not just dimensionality.
Storage cost scales linearly.
1M documents × 1 536 dims × 4 bytes (float32) = 6 GB just for the vectors. This is why vector databases use quantisation (int8, binary).
Curse of dimensionality.
In very high dimensions, distances between random points converge. Cosine similarity (angle, not distance) is preferred precisely because it's more stable at high D.
